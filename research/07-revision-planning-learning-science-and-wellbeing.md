# Revision planning, learning science and wellbeing

## Core evidence-informed model

The Learning Scientists summarise six useful strategies:

1. spaced practice;
2. retrieval practice;
3. elaboration;
4. interleaving;
5. concrete examples;
6. dual coding.

Their own guidance says the evidence is strongest for spacing and retrieval. Retrieval should be present in every study session; spacing and interleaving help decide when and what to revisit; elaboration, examples and dual coding can deepen learning. The materials also explicitly warn that strategies cannot guarantee grades.

The EEF’s 2025 metacognition guidance supports explicitly teaching students to **plan, monitor and evaluate** learning and to build knowledge about themselves, the task and possible strategies.

Together, these suggest a revision loop:

`diagnose → choose → retrieve → check → correct → apply → schedule next review → reflect`

## What the website should make students do

### Diagnose

- Start with low-stakes questions or a topic checklist rather than asking “How confident are you?” alone.
- Keep confidence and performance as separate measures; the gap reveals overconfidence or anxiety.
- Record errors by misconception/skill, not only by unit.

### Choose a manageable session

- One or two concrete outcomes, for example “recall five ownership types and answer one contextual comparison.”
- Recommend a duration that fits the student’s available time.
- Mix knowledge and application rather than promising to “finish a whole unit.”

### Retrieve before reviewing

- Blank-page recall, flash questions, formula recall, diagram labels, code tracing or document conventions from memory.
- Then compare with accurate feedback/source content.
- Re-reading becomes a correction tool, not the entire session.

### Correct

- Require the student to state why the original answer was wrong.
- Present a short correction and a near-transfer question.
- Add the misconception to a revisit queue.

### Apply

- Business/Enterprise: use a new business context and ask for a consequence or decision.
- Computer Science: trace/change/test an algorithm or apply a system concept to a scenario.
- iMedia: identify a convention, explain its effect, repair a weak planning document or choose a format for a purpose.

### Space the next review

An initial practical schedule could revisit after approximately 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days and 30 days, but adapt intervals to success, exam proximity and topic difficulty. This is a product heuristic, not a scientifically universal optimum.

### Interleave

- Mix similar concepts that students confuse: profit vs cash, RAM vs ROM, compiler vs interpreter, raster vs vector, primary vs secondary research.
- Alternate content recall and exam technique.
- Later in the course, mix topics across a paper rather than revising only in specification order.

### Reflect

Use three short prompts:

- What can I now do without help?
- What error still repeats?
- What will I revisit and when?

## Planning views

### Two-year map

- Show MEA’s actual Year 10/11 sequence.
- Overlay assessment windows, mocks and NEA milestones only after local confirmation.
- Explain dependencies: R068 before R069; R093 knowledge feeds R094/R095; Year 10 J277 content remains live in Year 11.

### Half-term map

- List current topics, prerequisite gaps and assessment tasks.
- Add catch-up paths after absence.
- Separate “learned in class,” “retrieved successfully” and “exam ready.”

### Weekly plan

- Available days/minutes.
- Upcoming assessments.
- A balanced queue generated from weak topics, overdue spaced reviews and required coursework skills.
- Protected rest and other subjects.

### Session plan

- 2-minute retrieval warm-up.
- 10–20 minutes focused retrieval/learning.
- 10–20 minutes applied or exam question.
- 5 minutes mark/correct.
- 1 minute schedule/reflection.

The duration should be adjustable. The method matters more than a branded timer.

## Subject-specific revision patterns

### Pearson Business

- Formula retrieval plus one contextual calculation and interpretation.
- Keyword → example → linked consequence.
- Timed 3/6/9/12-mark progression.
- Source-application drills that turn a case fact into a business effect.
- Mixed Theme 1/2 practice once Year 11 begins.

### Enterprise R067

- Topic Area retrieval and short scenario application.
- Financial viability mini-cases.
- Marketing-mix interdependence tasks.
- Eight-mark bullet-coverage practice.

### Enterprise R068/R069

- Teach and practise component skills on fictional/public sample briefs before live assessment.
- Keep live NEA work out of unsupervised revision features.
- Skill checks: research design, sampling, presenting results, design mix, calculation, brand coherence and pitch rehearsal.

### Computer Science

- Daily code/algorithm reading in small doses.
- Trace first, then predict, then modify, then write.
- Retrieval of precise component/protocol/legal definitions.
- Paired threat/control and feature/benefit tasks.
- Non-calculator binary/hex/file-size practice.
- Timed Paper 2 Section B tasks using the school language and OCR reference language.

### Creative iMedia

- Image/example recognition followed by an explanation of meaning/impact.
- Pre-production document convention retrieval.
- “Spot and improve” weak storyboard/script/wireframe tasks.
- Legal/regulator scenario cards.
- File-format and property selection.
- R094/R095 software skills practised on unrelated briefs, with process evidence and export checks.

## Avoid ineffective defaults

- Do not equate time logged with learning.
- Do not make highlighting/re-reading the main activity.
- Do not let a student endlessly repeat already-mastered easy items.
- Do not reward guessing until the correct answer appears.
- Do not show a confidence colour as if it were objective mastery.
- Do not recommend cramming or remove sleep/rest from a plan.
- Do not predict topics and hide unpredicted content.

## Wellbeing and exam stress

The NHS recommends a realistic schedule, manageable chunks, mixed subjects, past-paper familiarity, regular short breaks, physical activity, preparation for the day and asking a trusted person for help. The page also advises reading instructions, planning time and moving on temporarily when stuck.

The eventual site should therefore:

- let students reduce an overloaded plan automatically;
- never shame missed sessions or use punishing streak-loss language;
- include breaks, movement and rest;
- provide a visible “I feel overwhelmed” route to MEA support and trusted national services;
- distinguish ordinary exam nerves from a need to speak to a trusted adult/professional;
- keep wellbeing advice supportive and non-diagnostic.

## Exam-day rules

The current JCQ written-exam guidance says students should:

- arrive on time and follow MEA’s arrival instructions;
- write clearly in black ink unless the paper says otherwise;
- bring only permitted equipment in a transparent pencil case;
- not possess notes, phones, earbuds, smart glasses, tablets or watches in the exam room;
- not use correcting fluid/tape, erasable pens, gel pens or highlighters in answers;
- listen to the invigilator and report wrong/incomplete papers, missing access arrangements or illness immediately;
- not communicate with other candidates or borrow equipment;
- leave all exam stationery in the room.

For these courses specifically:

- Pearson Business and OCR Enterprise R067 permit a calculator.
- OCR J277 is non-calculator.
- R093 does not require a calculator under its assessment arrangements.

The active-year JCQ document and MEA exams guidance must always override this snapshot.
