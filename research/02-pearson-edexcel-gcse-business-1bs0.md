# Pearson Edexcel GCSE (9–1) Business — 1BS0

## Qualification at a glance

- Linear GCSE, single tier.
- Two externally marked papers in the same May/June series.
- Each paper: 1 hour 45 minutes, 90 marks, 50%.
- Calculator permitted.
- All questions are compulsory.
- Each paper has Section A (35 marks), Section B (30) and Section C (25).
- Question types include multiple choice, calculation, short answer and extended writing. Sections B and C use business contexts/source material.
- Assessment objectives across the qualification: AO1 knowledge and understanding 35%; AO2 application 35%; AO3 analysis and evaluation 30%.
- At least 10% of marks assess quantitative skills. Pearson’s assessment support states that each paper contains four calculate questions worth eight marks in total.
- Formulae are not supplied in the examination.

## Paper 1 — Investigating small business

Theme 1 is taught through the lens of starting and operating a small business.

### 1.1 Enterprise and entrepreneurship

#### 1.1.1 The dynamic nature of business

- New and changing business ideas.
- Changes in technology, customer wants and products/services becoming obsolete.
- How these changes create opportunities and threats.

#### 1.1.2 Risk and reward

- Risks: business failure, financial loss and lack of security.
- Rewards: business success, profit, independence and satisfaction.

#### 1.1.3 The role of business enterprise

- Producing goods or services.
- Meeting customer needs.
- Adding value: convenience, branding, quality, design and unique selling points.
- The role of entrepreneurship: organising resources, making decisions and taking risks.

### 1.2 Spotting a business opportunity

#### 1.2.1 Customer needs

- Price, quality, choice and convenience.
- Why identifying and understanding needs matters.

#### 1.2.2 Market research

- Purpose: identify/understand needs, identify gaps and reduce risk.
- Primary research methods: surveys, questionnaires, focus groups and observation.
- Secondary research sources: internet, market reports and government reports.
- Qualitative and quantitative data.
- Role of social media.
- Reliability of data: sample size, bias, question design and data currency.

#### 1.2.3 Market segmentation

- Segmenting by location, demographics, behaviour, lifestyle and income.
- How segmentation helps target customers and shape the marketing mix.

#### 1.2.4 The competitive environment

- Understanding competitors: strengths, weaknesses, price, quality, location, product range and customer service.
- Impact of competition on business decisions.
- Market mapping: price and quality axes, gaps and positioning.

### 1.3 Putting a business idea into practice

#### 1.3.1 Business aims and objectives

- Survival, profit, sales, market share, financial security and social objectives.
- Why aims differ and change.

#### 1.3.2 Business revenues, costs and profits

- Revenue, fixed costs, variable costs, total costs, profit and loss.
- Interest and interest rates.
- Break-even quantity and break-even charts.
- Margin of safety.
- Interpreting changes in price, costs and sales.

#### 1.3.3 Cash and cash flow

- Cash inflows and outflows.
- Net cash flow and opening/closing balances.
- Cash-flow forecasts.
- Difference between cash and profit.
- Causes and consequences of cash-flow problems and possible responses.

#### 1.3.4 Sources of business finance

- Short term: overdraft and trade credit.
- Long term: personal savings, venture capital, share capital, loans, retained profit and crowdfunding.
- Suitability depends on amount, purpose, cost, risk, ownership/control and time.

### 1.4 Making the business effective

#### 1.4.1 Ownership and liability

- Sole trader, partnership, private limited company and franchise.
- Limited and unlimited liability.
- Advantages/disadvantages of each form and why ownership may change.

#### 1.4.2 Business location

- Proximity to market, labour, materials and competitors.
- Nature of activity, impact of the internet, costs and infrastructure.

#### 1.4.3 The marketing mix

- Product, price, promotion and place.
- How the elements work together and must fit the target market/competitive environment.

#### 1.4.4 Business plans

- Content: idea, aims/objectives, market research, marketing, location, personnel and finance.
- Uses: reducing risk, planning, measuring progress and obtaining finance.
- Limitations: forecasts may be inaccurate and circumstances change.

### 1.5 Understanding external influences on business

#### 1.5.1 Stakeholders

- Owners, employees, customers, suppliers, local community and government.
- Objectives, influence, conflict and interdependence.

#### 1.5.2 Technology and business

- E-commerce, social media, digital communication and payment systems.
- Effects on sales, costs, location, marketing, productivity and customer expectations.

#### 1.5.3 Legislation and business

- Consumer law, employment law and health and safety.
- Effects on costs, training, processes, product standards and reputation.

#### 1.5.4 The economy and business

- Consumer income, unemployment, inflation, exchange rates, taxation and interest rates.
- Consequences for costs, demand, finance and decisions.

#### 1.5.5 External influences

- How businesses respond to technology, legislation and the economy.
- Importance of flexible decisions and the limits of control.

## Paper 2 — Building a business

Theme 2 focuses on how a business develops beyond its start-up phase. Theme 1 knowledge can be assumed and applied.

### 2.1 Growing the business

#### 2.1.1 Business growth

- Organic growth: new products and new markets.
- Inorganic growth: merger and takeover.
- Economies and diseconomies of scale.
- Changing aims/objectives as businesses grow.
- Public limited companies and stock-market flotation.
- Sources of finance for growth.

#### 2.1.2 Changes in business aims and objectives

- Market conditions, technology, performance, legislation and internal change.
- How objectives may shift between survival, growth, profit, market share, ethics and shareholder value.

#### 2.1.3 Business and globalisation

- Imports and exports.
- Changing business locations and international markets.
- Barriers to trade and trade blocs.
- How globalisation affects competition, supply chains and decisions.

#### 2.1.4 Ethics, environment and business

- Environmental pressure, sustainability and ethical sourcing/behaviour.
- Trade-offs among profit, cost, reputation and stakeholder expectations.

#### 2.1.5 Expansion into international markets

- Reasons and methods for selling abroad.
- Adapting the marketing mix to different cultures, incomes and regulations.
- Benefits and drawbacks of multinational operation.

### 2.2 Making marketing decisions

#### 2.2.1 Product

- Design mix: function, aesthetics and cost.
- Product life cycle: development, introduction, growth, maturity and decline.
- Extension strategies.
- Differentiation and adding value.

#### 2.2.2 Price

- Cost-plus, competitive, penetration, skimming and promotional pricing.
- Factors influencing the choice of strategy.

#### 2.2.3 Promotion

- Advertising, sponsorship, product trials, special offers and branding.
- Above/below-the-line approaches and changes caused by technology.
- Choosing methods for the objective, audience, product and budget.

#### 2.2.4 Place

- Distribution channels, retailers, wholesalers, e-commerce and multi-channel distribution.

#### 2.2.5 Using the marketing mix to make decisions

- Interdependence of the four Ps.
- How technology, competition and target market alter the mix.

### 2.3 Making operational decisions

#### 2.3.1 Business operations

- Job, batch and flow production.
- Balancing productivity, flexibility, quality and cost.
- Effects of technology on production.

#### 2.3.2 Working with suppliers

- Stock control, buffer stock and just in time.
- Procurement, logistics and managing suppliers.
- Factors affecting supplier choice: price, quality, reliability, capacity, trust and location.

#### 2.3.3 Managing quality

- Quality control and quality assurance.
- Benefits/costs of quality and consequences of poor quality.

#### 2.3.4 The sales process

- Product knowledge, speed/efficiency, customer engagement, response to feedback and after-sales service.
- Impact on repeat purchase, reputation and revenue.

### 2.4 Making financial decisions

#### 2.4.1 Business calculations

- Gross profit and net profit.
- Gross profit margin and net profit margin.
- Average rate of return.

#### 2.4.2 Understanding business performance

- Interpreting quantitative data, financial statements and ratios.
- Comparing performance over time or with competitors.
- Limitations of quantitative evidence and importance of context.

### 2.5 Making human resource decisions

#### 2.5.1 Organisational structures

- Hierarchical and flat structures.
- Centralisation and decentralisation.
- Span of control, chain of command and delegation.
- Effects on communication, motivation, decision speed and costs.

#### 2.5.2 Effective recruitment

- Internal/external recruitment.
- Job analysis, job descriptions and person specifications.
- Application forms, CVs and interviews.

#### 2.5.3 Effective training and development

- Induction, on-the-job and off-the-job training.
- Costs and benefits of training.

#### 2.5.4 Motivation

- Financial: remuneration, bonus, commission and promotion.
- Non-financial: job rotation, job enrichment, autonomy and training.
- Importance for productivity, retention and customer service.

## Formula bank

Students must know and apply these without a supplied formula sheet:

- `total costs = total fixed costs + total variable costs`
- `sales revenue = selling price × quantity sold`
- `profit = sales revenue − total costs`
- `break-even output = fixed costs ÷ (selling price per unit − variable cost per unit)`
- `break-even revenue = break-even output × selling price`
- `margin of safety = actual or budgeted sales − break-even sales`
- `interest rate (%) = total interest paid ÷ amount borrowed × 100`
- `net cash flow = total inflows − total outflows`
- `closing balance = opening balance + net cash flow`
- `gross profit = sales revenue − cost of sales`
- `gross profit margin = gross profit ÷ sales revenue × 100`
- `net profit = gross profit − other operating expenses and interest`
- `net profit margin = net profit ÷ sales revenue × 100`
- `average rate of return = average annual profit ÷ cost of investment × 100`

The site should include formula recall, substitution, units/rounding, reverse calculations, chart interpretation and business interpretation—not calculation drills alone.

## Assessment design implications

- Every specification point remains examinable; “predicted topics” should not drive revision.
- Retrieval should mix Theme 1 and Theme 2 once Theme 2 begins because Paper 2 relies on foundational knowledge.
- Extended-response practice needs both reasoning and authentic context application.
- A source-highlighting tool should distinguish evidence that is merely copied from evidence that is used to develop a business consequence.
- Formula practice should be interleaved with “what does the result mean for this business?” questions.
- Historical boundaries may be visualised for orientation but must be labelled by series and never converted into a guaranteed target.
