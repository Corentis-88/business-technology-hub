# Exam technique and common errors

This document distinguishes official marking evidence from popular teacher structures. A mnemonic can help a student remember to reason, but it is never a mark-scheme requirement unless the awarding body says so.

## Universal habits

1. Read the command word, mark value, named organisation/product and every bullet.
2. Underline the decision or outcome the question is really asking about.
3. Recall precise subject knowledge before looking for context details.
4. Use context to change the reasoning, not as a decorative name drop.
5. Build consequences with explicit links: because → leads to → therefore → effect on the named objective/stakeholder.
6. Check that the final sentence answers the question rather than repeating the opening.
7. For calculations, show substitution and units; follow rounding instructions.
8. Attempt every question and return to difficult items if time remains.
9. Leave enough room/time for the highest-tariff responses.

A reasonable starting timing heuristic is roughly one minute per mark plus reading/checking time, adjusted for the paper. It is not an awarding-body rule, and students should test it in timed practice.

## Pearson Business command words

### Give / Define — 1 mark

- Give one correct item or a precise meaning.
- Context is normally not needed unless the question asks for it.
- Do not add a contradictory second idea.

### State — 1 mark

- When the named business is in the question, use a relevant applied fact.

### Identify — 1 mark

- Read the graph/table/source and select the requested information.

### Calculate — usually 2 marks

- A correct final answer on the answer line receives both marks.
- If the final answer is wrong, clear working may earn method credit.
- A formula alone earns no mark in the published guidance.
- Check units, percentages and specified decimal places/significant figures.

### Outline — 2 marks

- One correct point plus a developed/applied detail.
- Pearson repeatedly reports lost marks where the response remains generic despite a named context.

### Explain — 3 marks

- A valid point followed by two linked strands of explanation.
- In Section A, context is not required.
- Three focused sentences can be enough; definitions and restating the question do not build explanation.
- Answer the exact focus: a “method” is not the same as a “benefit.”

#### MEA’s BLT method — Pearson Business only

MEA teaches each three-mark explanation as **one BLT**:

- **Because** — place `because` after the initial business point to explain why it is true or relevant. **“Because” must never start a sentence.**
- **Leads To** — develop the immediate business consequence.
- **Therefore** — complete the chain with its resulting effect on the business, customer or stakeholder.

The required pattern is:

> **[Business point] because [reason]. This leads to [consequence]. Therefore, [final business effect].**

The answer does not have to repeat every sentence stem mechanically, but all three logical links should be visible and no sentence may begin with “Because.” For example:

> Training could improve customer service **because** employees will know how to handle complaints. This **leads to** customers being more satisfied. **Therefore**, repeat purchases and sales revenue may increase.

The eventual website must:

- teach BLT explicitly in the Pearson Business 1BS0 area;
- label one complete BLT as the expected structure for one three-mark answer;
- highlight the Because, Leads To and Therefore links in worked examples;
- require the business point before `because` and reject or correct any sentence beginning with “Because”;
- diagnose which BLT link is missing when giving feedback;
- use BLT as a reasoning building block in larger Business answers where appropriate, while adding the application, balance and judgement required by the higher-tariff command word;
- never present BLT as the required structure for Enterprise J837, Computer Science J277 or Creative iMedia J834.

BLT is MEA’s teaching method rather than wording required by Pearson’s mark scheme. The website should preserve that distinction while consistently reinforcing the school method.

### Discuss — 6 marks

- Assesses developed business knowledge and analysis in a non-context Section A item.
- Build a balanced or multi-faceted chain using relevant consequences.
- No separate evaluation judgement is required for these marks.
- Pearson guidance often illustrates two developed paragraphs; paragraph count itself is not marked.

### Analyse — 6 marks

- Apply the source/business throughout and build developed consequences.
- A generic chain is capped well below full marks; merely copying a source phrase is not developed application.
- Link the effect to an appropriate objective such as profit, cash flow, sales, market share, productivity, cost, reputation or survival.

### Justify — 9 marks

- Make and support a choice in context.
- Analyse relevant consequences and evaluate why the chosen option fits this business.
- Pearson confirms that a student can reach full marks by developing one option; both options are not compulsory.
- A clear judgement should be based on factors, time horizon or conditions rather than assertion.

### Evaluate — 12 marks

- Show accurate knowledge, sustained contextual application, developed analysis and evaluation.
- Consider relevant advantages/limitations or different effects, but avoid mechanically writing generic “pros and cons.”
- The conclusion must add evaluative value: decide, explain the decisive factor, acknowledge a condition/time horizon and connect it to the context.
- Repeating earlier sentences is not a developed conclusion.

### Frequent Pearson Business errors

- Predicting topics and leaving gaps when every topic is examinable.
- Repeating the question rather than starting a chain.
- Copying case material without using it.
- Generic advantages/disadvantages.
- Not learning formulae.
- Giving both options superficially in a nine-marker when one developed option would be stronger.
- Ending a twelve-marker with “it depends” but never stating what it depends on or which decision follows.

## OCR Enterprise R067

### Short and medium responses

- Use exact enterprise vocabulary.
- If the question names a person/product/market, make every relevant point fit it.
- Distinguish “what it is” from “why it is useful.”
- For financial questions, calculate accurately and interpret the result for viability/cash/decision-making.
- For method questions, name the actual method (for example questionnaire) rather than only the channel (online).

### Eight-mark level-of-response item

- Read and address each bullet in the question.
- Use correct Topic Area 4 knowledge.
- Apply to the developing context at least once and preferably throughout the reasoning.
- Explain effects and trade-offs, then reach a clear context-based conclusion where requested.
- Avoid unsupported stereotypes about customers.

### Frequent R067 errors

- Treating the four Ps as independent lists rather than an interdependent mix.
- Defining break-even when asked how it helps a decision.
- Vague “it will attract customers” claims with no mechanism or target segment.
- Missing a bullet or leaving questions blank.
- Confusing profit with cash.
- Naming support without explaining what the provider does.

## OCR Computer Science J277

### Knowledge questions

- Give a definition when asked for a definition; an example alone may not score.
- Use the correct device/component/protocol and its specific role.
- Avoid vague claims such as “it is faster” without the data path, process or user effect.

### Paper 1 eight-mark level-of-response

- Cover all named bullet areas.
- Use technical knowledge accurately.
- Analyse positive and negative implications for relevant stakeholders.
- Reach a justified recommendation/conclusion if the question asks for one.
- Structure is flexible; quality, coverage and reasoning are marked, not a memorised paragraph count.

### Paper 2 six-mark response

- This is point-based rather than the same level-of-response grid as Paper 1.
- Aim for the requested number/range of precise, developed technical points.
- Do not pad with an introduction or conclusion when the task asks for explanations.

### Algorithm Design / Write / Test / Refine

- **Design:** a clear flowchart, pseudocode or other accepted representation can be used where allowed.
- **Write/Refine:** use OCR Exam Reference Language or a high-level language; follow the required parameter names, inputs and outputs.
- **Test:** choose normal, boundary, invalid and erroneous data as relevant; state expected results.
- **Refine:** change the logic that causes the stated failure; do not rewrite unrelated code.
- Trace manually with a variable table before finalising.
- Prefer the simplest correct solution.

### Frequent J277 errors

- Off-by-one boundaries and wrong comparison operators.
- Failing to initialise/update a total, counter or flag.
- Using new user input instead of a supplied parameter.
- Reversing assignment.
- Incorrect indentation/nesting that changes logic.
- Confusing sample rate and bit depth.
- Confusing network hardware, addresses or protocol purposes.
- Calling testing a maintainability feature.

## OCR Creative iMedia R093

### Short responses

- Learn the complete R093 content; NEA experience does not cover every examinable term.
- Name the convention/body/role and state its precise effect or responsibility.
- Use media vocabulary such as hierarchy, focal point, connotation, shot type, resolution, vector and compression accurately.

### Explaining a media code

1. Name the code and the specific instance.
2. Explain the meaning or impression it creates.
3. Connect that effect to the named audience, purpose or product.

“Red attracts attention” is usually too shallow; explain what attention is directed to and why that matters in this product.

### Improving a pre-production document

- Make or describe a specific practical improvement.
- Explain how it helps the named user/job role perform a production task.
- Address multiple relevant conventions/content gaps for higher-level responses.
- Do not merely say “add detail”; state which detail, where and what production decision it enables.

### Nine-mark suitability discussion

- Use the bullets printed in the question.
- Judge suitability for the named user/purpose/context.
- Use accurate document/product conventions and developed reasoning.
- OCR removed an old sample-mark-scheme line requiring an explicit range of strengths and weaknesses. Balanced thinking may still be useful, but it is not a standalone wording requirement from January 2025 onward.

### Frequent R093 errors

- “Faster/easier/better” with no explained mechanism.
- Weak knowledge of job roles, regulators, location recce, physical distribution and vector properties.
- Describing rather than discussing suitability.
- Naming media codes without their meaning/impact.
- Using generic audience stereotypes.
- Missing sections of a multipart extended response.

## From public teacher videos and platforms

Recurring third-party advice is broadly consistent with official reports:

- use retrieval before re-reading;
- practise questions immediately after learning a topic;
- mark with the official scheme and rewrite weak answers;
- use linked reasoning and context in Business/Enterprise;
- hand-trace and regularly write code in Computer Science;
- use visual examples and repair weak documents in iMedia.

MEA’s BLT method is the explicit Pearson Business scaffold and should be taught consistently in that qualification. Other branded structures such as PEEL should not be introduced unless MEA requests them. The site should show how BLT maps to Pearson’s linked explanation requirements and allow the visible sentence stems to fade as students become independent, while continuing to check the underlying reasoning chain.

## Feedback model for the future site

Feedback should identify the precise missing layer:

- **K:** knowledge incorrect or missing;
- **A:** application absent/generic;
- **R:** reasoning link missing;
- **J:** judgement unsupported;
- **C:** calculation/method/unit error;
- **Q:** command word or question focus missed;
- **E:** evidence/convention not demonstrated in practical work.

That is more actionable than only displaying a mark.
