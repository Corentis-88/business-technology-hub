# OCR Level 1/Level 2 Cambridge National in Creative iMedia — J834

## Qualification at a glance

- Technical Award available in England; included on Key Stage 4 performance tables.
- 120 guided learning hours; approximately 155 total qualification time hours.
- Two mandatory units plus one optional unit.
- R093 Creative iMedia in the media industry: 48 GLH, 70 raw marks/80 UMS, 40%.
- R094 Visual identity and digital graphics: 30 GLH, 50 marks/UMS, 25%.
- MEA option R095 Characters and comics: 42 GLH, 70 marks/UMS, 35%.
- Grades: D*2, D2, M2, P2, D1, M1, P1 and U.
- Fixed overall UMS boundaries out of 200: 180, 160, 140, 120, 100, 80 and 60 respectively.
- The exam is terminal: R093 must be sat in the final assessment series; R094 and R095 must be submitted before or in that series.

## R093 — Creative iMedia in the media industry

### Assessment format

- 1 hour 30 minutes; 70 marks.
- Part A: 10 marks, normally 7–10 closed, multiple-choice or short questions sampling all topic areas and primarily recalling knowledge.
- Part B: 60 marks based on one media scenario.
- Part B combines closed/short responses and three extended responses. Recent structure includes a six-mark explanation and two nine-mark level-of-response questions, but students should follow the live paper rather than assume an invariant order.
- Around one minute per mark leaves roughly 20 minutes for reading, planning and checking.

### Topic Area 1 — The media industry

#### Media sectors and products

- Traditional and new media.
- Sectors/products such as publishing, television, film, radio/audio, advertising, websites, interactive media, games, animation, digital graphics and social/socially distributed media.
- Convergence: one product or platform may combine text, image, audio, video and interaction.
- Platforms and audiences change how products are designed and distributed.

#### Job roles

- Creative roles, technical roles and senior/management roles.
- Typical responsibilities across pre-production, production and post-production.
- Examples include writer, designer, illustrator, photographer, animator, editor, camera/sound roles, developer and director/producer/project-management roles.
- Students must know what a named role actually does, in which production phase and why it matters to the product.

### Topic Area 2 — Factors influencing product design

#### Purpose, style, content and layout

- Purposes include inform, educate, entertain, advertise/promote and influence.
- Style must suit purpose, genre, client and audience.
- Content selection and layout create clarity, hierarchy, engagement and meaning.

#### Client requirements

- Explicit and implicit requirements.
- Content, style, audience, purpose, format, deadline, budget and constraints in a brief.
- Interpret rather than repeat the brief.

#### Audience demographics and segmentation

- Age, gender, location, income, education, occupation, interests, lifestyle and other relevant characteristics.
- Avoid stereotypes; explain how a known audience characteristic changes a design decision.

#### Research

- Primary and secondary sources.
- Qualitative and quantitative data.
- Reliability, validity, relevance, currency, bias and permissions.
- Use research to support design choices.

#### Media codes

Technical codes:

- Camera shot type, angle and movement.
- Lighting.
- Editing, transitions and pace.
- Audio, speech, music and sound effects.
- Interactivity/navigation.

Symbolic codes:

- Colour and colour connotations.
- Mise-en-scène: setting, props, costume, gesture and composition.
- Character and cultural conventions.

Written codes:

- Typography, captions, headlines, labels, slogans, dialogue and narrative.

Students must explain the meaning, impact or engagement created for the given audience—not merely name a code.

### Topic Area 3 — Pre-production planning

#### Work plans

- Tasks/activities.
- Timescales, milestones and deadlines.
- Resources, personnel and responsibilities.
- Contingencies and dependencies.
- Production phases.

#### Ideas-generation documents

- Mind maps: structure and develop linked ideas.
- Mood boards: communicate visual/style inspiration.
- Purpose, conventions, strengths, limitations and intended users.

#### Product-design documents

- Asset log.
- Flowchart.
- Script.
- Storyboard.
- Visualisation diagram.
- Wireframe layout.

For each, students need:

- correct conventions and content;
- purpose and suitable production use;
- likely user/job role;
- ability to identify and explain improvements in context.

#### Legal and regulatory considerations

- Privacy, permission and consent.
- Defamation.
- Data protection.
- Intellectual property and copyright.
- Creative Commons and fair dealing at the required level.
- Advertising Standards Authority.
- Ofcom.
- British Board of Film Classification.
- PEGI.
- Health and safety, risk assessment and location reconnaissance/recce.

Students must know what a body/law does and how it changes production or distribution.

### Topic Area 4 — Distribution considerations

- Online and physical distribution platforms.
- Suitability for audience, product, reach, cost and technical constraints.
- Digital image properties and formats.
- Audio and video properties/formats.
- Resolution, dimensions, colour depth, sample/bit rates, frame rate and other relevant properties.
- Lossy/lossless compression, file size, quality and purpose.
- Select a format for editing/native work versus final distribution.

## R094 — Visual identity and digital graphics

### Assessment conditions

- Mandatory OCR-set NEA, centre assessed and externally moderated.
- 50 marks over two tasks; normally 10–12 GLH.
- Task 1 is 20 marks; Task 2 is 30 marks in the current sample assignment.

### Topic Area 1 — Develop visual identity

- Purpose of visual identity: recognition, differentiation, consistency, values and audience relationship.
- Components: name, logo, colour palette, typography, imagery, graphics and style.
- Elements and styles used to create an identity.
- Interpret the client and target audience.
- Research and justify suitable choices.

### Topic Area 2 — Plan digital graphics

- Graphic-design concepts and conventions: composition, hierarchy, alignment, balance, contrast, proximity, white space and consistency.
- Raster and vector concepts.
- Asset sourcing, ownership, permission and recording sources.
- Dimensions, resolution, colour mode and file formats.
- Planning documents, layout and asset use.

### Topic Area 3 — Create digital graphics

- Create/source and prepare assets.
- Use image-editing tools and techniques: selections, transforms, cropping, masks, layers, opacity/blending, colour adjustment, filters/effects, retouching and typography as appropriate.
- Combine assets into a coherent graphic.
- Maintain image quality and fitness for purpose.
- Save an editable/native version and export the required distribution version with suitable properties.
- Capture evidence of technical processes where the finished image alone cannot show them.

### Current MEA delivery

MEA’s Year 10 map names Photoshop. Home-practice content should offer concept-first instruction plus clearly labelled Photoshop steps; free-tool alternatives must not silently diverge from school menus/workflows.

### Moderator lessons

- Generic office software may limit opportunities to show the range of image-editing skill expected for higher bands.
- Explain independent choices and application to the brief/audience.
- Technical-process evidence matters; a final graphic alone may not prove how it was made.
- Export properties must match the stated use.

## R095 — Characters and comics

MEA’s public maps confirm this is the chosen optional unit.

### Assessment conditions

- OCR-set NEA, centre assessed and externally moderated.
- 70 marks over three tasks; normally 12–15 GLH.
- Task 1: 20 marks; Task 2: 34 marks; Task 3: 16 marks in the current sample assignment.
- Students create an original character and a multiple-page comic.

### Topic Area 1 / Task 1 — Plan characters and comics

#### Character conventions

- Character types and roles, including hero, villain, supporting and other genre roles.
- Cartoon, doodle, photorealistic, manga and other relevant visual styles.
- Physical and non-physical characteristics.
- Tropes, personality and backstory.
- Facial features, expression, pose and body language to communicate emotion/personality.
- Originality and suitability for genre, audience and client.

#### Comic conventions

- Colour and typography.
- Text, sound words/onomatopoeia, captions and narration.
- Speech, thought and other communication bubbles.
- Focal points, foreground and background.
- Panel size, shape, borders and gutters.
- Splash pages, spreads, rule of thirds and staggered layouts.
- Sequencing, story flow, pace and passage of time.
- Genre and audience conventions.

#### Planning

- Interpret the client brief and audience rather than restating them.
- Character profiles.
- Character sketches/drafts, thumbnails, physical models for capture or digital drafts.
- Plot, story arc and narrative structure.
- Script conventions.
- Storyboard and panel/shot types.
- Resource/software planning.
- Asset list/table including ownership/permission and an explanation of where, how and why each asset contributes.
- There is no requirement for a Gantt chart in R095; planning marks are about creative/product planning.

### Topic Area 2 / Task 2 — Create characters and comics

- Use appropriate software tools to create an original character.
- Create/edit assets, backgrounds, text elements and panel content.
- Build components as suitable editable assets before combining them.
- Translate the script/storyboard into effective panel sequence and story flow.
- Use a wide range of purposeful technical skills rather than effects for their own sake.
- Keep native/editable files.
- Export character and comic for the required print/digital distribution, with correct dimensions, resolution and format.
- Record technical process evidence without turning it into a generic step-by-step tutorial.

OCR’s R095 guidance lists viable image-editing options including Photoshop, GIMP, Krita, Photopea, Pixlr, Affinity Photo and others; comic assembly options include Photoshop, Photopea, Canva and Comic Life. Suitability depends on whether the chosen tool can demonstrate the specification skills.

### Topic Area 3 / Task 3 — Review characters and comics

- Create/use a checklist to test technical properties and comic/character conventions.
- Review fitness for the client, purpose and audience.
- Review aesthetics, readability, engagement and story flow.
- Identify constraints and specific improvements to existing work.
- Distinguish improvements from further developments such as a sequel, serialization, themed edition, new character or spin-off.
- Explain proposed changes; the task does not require students to implement all improvements/developments.

### Moderator lessons for R095

- A copied/restated brief is not an interpretation.
- Character planning needs depth; it is a frequent weakness.
- An asset table that only lists sources is not enough—explain contribution and use.
- The character must be original. Editing a premade character restricts evidence and higher-band access.
- Show technical process, not just the final output.
- Use comic conventions and varied panels; a narrated storyboard-like layout is not automatically an effective comic.
- Judge fitness for the specific brief and audience, not only whether it looks creative.
- Export an accessible product with correct properties.
- Test technical properties in Task 3, not only completion against the brief.
- Separate “improve what exists” from “develop something additional in future.”

## What the 2025 R093 examiner report highlights

Successful answers are precise, specific and contextual. Common problems include:

- vague phrases such as “faster” or “easier” without explaining what becomes faster/easier and why it matters;
- relying on practical NEA familiarity instead of learning the complete R093 content;
- weak knowledge of ethos, job responsibilities, regulatory bodies, physical media, vector graphics and location recce;
- naming a media code but not explaining audience meaning/impact;
- repeating the scenario rather than applying knowledge;
- failing to use the command word.

For questions asking students to improve a pre-production document, the student should make a clear, practical change and explain how it helps the named user. For nine-mark suitability questions, OCR removed an old sample-mark-scheme requirement to explicitly list strengths and weaknesses; the live requirement from January 2025 is to discuss suitability using the bullets and context.

## Product implications

- The site must combine knowledge retrieval with visual examples and document-improvement practice.
- “Name the media code” and “explain its effect” should be separate skill steps.
- Interactive pre-production labs can let students annotate or repair a storyboard, script, wireframe, mood board and asset log.
- R094/R095 skill tutorials must be labelled as teaching/practice, never as answers to a live assignment.
- Asset/source tracking should be practised using fictional briefs and public sample assignments.
- R095 needs a visual glossary of panels, gutters, bubbles, shot types, typography and story flow.
