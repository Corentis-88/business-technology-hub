The live-paper median is about 218 words. A safe MEA target is therefore 200-230 words for a main source extract, rather than the 60-80 word summaries previously used.

Across these eight live extracts, the mean is 212.6 words, 12 sentences and 3.1 paragraphs. Paper 1 and Paper 2 are almost identical in narrative length (211.5 versus 213.8 words), but not in numerical density: Paper 1 averages about 2.25 numeric tokens per extract, while Paper 2 averages about 8.25. This fits the recurring distinction between smaller, person-led Theme 1 businesses and larger established Theme 2 organisations with growth, market-share, finance, operations and workforce data.

The SAM was also checked but is not used as the sole writing template. Its four narratives average about 150 words and are more visually dense. The live 2023-2024 pages give a more reliable current length and rhythm.

## What makes the wording recognisably Pearson-like

- The extract opens with a neutral identification of the business: what it sells, where it operates, its ownership or scale, and a short piece of history.
- Information is normally organised into three or four short paragraphs, not a list of answer clues.
- Sentences are direct and factual. Most contain one main idea, with occasional definitions or explanations of an unfamiliar product or process.
- The source mixes background facts with evidence that matters to the questions. It does not label facts as strengths, weaknesses, arguments or conclusions.
- Quantitative evidence is selective. Some extracts contain only a few useful numbers; others contain a dense cluster of dates, prices, percentages, capacity, debt, revenue or competitor information.
- A real decision tension is present: growth versus risk, quality versus cost, price versus demand, productivity versus employees, or convenience versus capacity.
- The source leaves reasoning for the student. It supplies facts but does not state the likely impact on sales, costs, profit, cash flow, reputation or success.
- Visual material is part of the evidence rather than decoration. Across the papers this includes photographs, review screenshots, comparison graphics, tables, charts and product/site images.
- Section B usually gives one Extract A page for Questions 4-6. Section C gives one Extract B page for Question 7 and often carries denser evidence for Justify and Evaluate.

Every 2023-2024 live source page contains one embedded illustrative photograph. Across the four papers, the related question pages add ten separate data displays: eight tables and two charts. Reviews are authentic in the SAM but do not recur in the eight live extracts, so they should be an occasional practice figure rather than a fixed template.

The eight live extracts typically contain about 9-14 usable fact clusters. Only around 2-4 are secondary to the final 9- or 12-mark question, and most of those still support an earlier question. MEA cases should therefore use soft distractors - such as a relevant date, product example or owner background - rather than random filler.

## Question relationship to the source

The specification fixes both papers at three sections and 90 marks. Sections B and C use business contexts. The recent papers repeatedly use this ladder:

- Section B: applied Outline (2), two applied Analyse questions (6 each), then a two-option Justify (9), with shorter questions/calculations around them.
- Section C: short applied questions, a two-option Justify (9), and an Evaluate question (12) ending with the instruction to use the supplied information and business knowledge.
- Discuss (6) appears in Section A and does not assess application to a supplied case.
- Analyse, Justify and Evaluate require the supplied context to be worked through the reasoning rather than copied or named.

The cross-series wording is stable. Each Section B has two contextual Analyse questions. Each paper has two Justify questions, one in Section B and one in Section C; all present exactly two options. Every live Evaluate question ends with the instruction to use the information provided as well as business knowledge.

## Copyright-safe MEA writing blueprint

Every MEA practice source must be original and clearly labelled as fictional practice, while matching the assessment structure:

1. Write 200-230 words in three or four factual paragraphs.
2. Begin with business identity, product/service, ownership/scale and location/history.
3. Add one operational or customer issue, one financial or numerical feature, and one competitive/external factor.
4. Include one or two figures such as an original chart, customer comment, comparison table or simple illustration.
5. Mix decisive evidence with plausible background detail; never bold or pre-label the "correct" evidence in the source.
6. Give the source a small question ladder that mirrors its section: Outline/Analyse/Justify for Section B or short application/Justify/Evaluate for Section C.
7. Keep any model answer separate from the source. Highlight how evidence becomes reasoning only after the student has inspected the neutral source.
8. Never reproduce Pearson text, business combinations, photographs, screenshots, tables or numerical sets. Do not use Pearson logos or claim that an MEA case is an official paper.
